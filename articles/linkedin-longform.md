**I gave an AI my entire digital life. The first question everyone asks is the right one.**

"What happens when it leaks?"

Good. That's the question that actually matters — and it's the one almost no one building personal agents wants to answer. So let me answer it first, then tell you what happened.

Three weeks ago I gave an agent I built — I call it Adam — years of my texts, emails, calendar, documents, and contacts. Since then my screen time has dropped 78%. Not because I forced it. Because Adam started doing the work, and I stopped needing to look.

But the 78% isn't the point of this post. The trust model is.

**Why I think this is safe to say out loud**

For ten years I built interfaces — three of them as an engineer at Meta, then spatial experiences for Lamborghini, Ferrari, and Aston Martin on Apple Vision Pro. I've shipped a lot of screens. I've come to believe most of them exist for one reason: we don't trust machines, so we keep a human in the loop to check the work. The screen is the supervision layer.

You cannot remove that layer with better hardware. You remove it with an agent reliable enough — and *auditable* enough — that checking becomes unnecessary. That distinction is everything, especially if you operate in a regulated industry where "the AI did something and we can't explain why" is a career-ending sentence.

**Two kinds of agent — and only one is defensible**

Most "AI agents" today are *app-navigating*: they mimic a human tapping through your apps. That's what the Humane Pin and Rabbit R1 promised. Both failed — Rabbit so completely that reviewers found little evidence its much-hyped "large action model" was doing anything at all. The retrospective consensus was blunt: AI doesn't need a new gadget; it needs to make the tools you already have work better.

Adam is the other kind: *corpus-native*. It doesn't open apps. It reads my actual life and answers from memory. And critically — every answer carries its provenance. When Adam tells me something, it shows me whether that came from an email, a text, or a calendar entry.

That's the part that makes this safe to scale: **a corpus-native agent that cites its sources is more auditable than a human assistant, not less.** You can trace every decision to its evidence. For anyone accountable for what their systems do, that's not a nice-to-have — it's the precondition for using AI on anything that matters.

**What "it works" actually looks like**

I'll publish the full data and method (link in the comments), including an honest accounting of what's reproducible today and what still requires infrastructure most people don't have. But the proof I care most about is one 60-second demonstration: I ask Adam something no single app could answer — it synthesizes an iMessage, an email, and a calendar event, gives the answer, and shows me the source of each. No app opened. Every claim traceable.

That single interaction is the whole thesis: reliable memory + verifiable provenance is what finally lets you stop watching the machine.

**Where this goes**

This is the problem I'm now building against at Mitosis Labs — the infrastructure for agents that are reliable, replicable, and verifiable enough that organizations can actually hand them real work. Not a new device. A trust layer.

The future isn't more interfaces to manage your attention. It's intelligence you can audit well enough to stop managing at all.

If you want the methodology and the replication kit, it's linked below. And if you run AI anywhere near a regulated process, I'd genuinely like to hear: what would you need to *see* before you'd trust an agent with it?

— PJ
